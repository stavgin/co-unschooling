Why the First 5 Years Are So Important! 🧠

Research shows that 85% of a child's brain is developed by age 5 — making these early years the most powerful window for growth, learning, and connection.

🏃
Physical Development

From a newborn's first grip to a 5-year-old doing somersaults — physical growth in the early years is truly amazing!

🌱 Birth – 3 Months ▼
Newborns build on their grasping and sucking reflexes. A key milestone is head control — and tummy time helps build strength in the neck, back, and arms!
🌿 3 – 6 Months ▼
Babies develop greater strength — rolling over, sitting with support, reaching for toys, and bringing objects to their mouths. Provide colourful, varied toys!
🌻 6 – 9 Months ▼
Children become more mobile! They sit without support, transfer objects between hands — and many start to crawl! 🐛
🌳 9 – 12 Months ▼
Babies stand unassisted, take first steps, pick up and throw objects, and develop fine motor skills like pincer grip (thumb + finger).
🚀 1 – 5 Years ▼
Running, jumping, riding a scooter, cutting with scissors, printing letters, doing somersaults — every year brings exciting new abilities! Give them space, time, and safe objects to explore.
🧠
Cognitive Development

A child's brain is building at lightning speed. Here's what's happening inside that curious little mind!

👀 Birth – 3 Months ▼
Newborns can see faces clearly, detect pitch and volume, see colours, and show facial expressions. They're taking in the world from day one!
😊 3 – 6 Months ▼
Babies recognise familiar faces and voices, respond to expressions, and start imitating sounds and facial movements — the beginning of communication!
🎈 6 – 9 Months ▼
Babies understand differences between animate and inanimate objects, and use size to judge distance. They gaze longer at surprising things — like balloons floating in midair! 🎈
🪄 9 – 12 Months ▼
A huge milestone: object permanence — understanding that things exist even when out of sight. This can trigger separation anxiety but is a sign of healthy brain development!
💡 1 – 5 Years ▼
From sorting objects and pretend play at age 2, to asking "why?" constantly at 4 — children are building cognitive schemas, understanding past and future, and getting ready for school!

Are You Okay Daddy?

At just two years of age, a child can show the wonderful emotion of empathy

🎯 Ages: 1–3 Years
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ Parenting with empathy and emotional guidance encourages healthy emotional growth.
  • ✅ Children are some of the best copycats — they copy the ways they see you treat people.
  • ✅ Provide simple, clear explanations about how other people feel when they are sad or hurt.
  • ✅ Praise your child's early expressions of empathy — they are wonderful signs of learning to care about others.
  • ✅ Don't expect empathy every time. Young children are still learning. Encourage it, but don't expect perfection.

Empathy in Baby Steps

We may all be born with a natural bent toward empathy. Babies start at birth with the ability to respond to the emotions of others — imitating the facial expressions of their caregivers or becoming distressed if they hear another baby cry. By three months, babies respond differently to happy faces than to sad faces, showing they can already tell the difference between emotions.

From Hugs to Band-Aids

Around a child's first birthday, something almost magical happens — a child begins to show concern for others. Between 13–15 months, more than half of children try to hug, pat, or touch another person showing distress. By 18–20 months, children show more varied ways of helping — verbal responses like 'Are you okay?', sharing toys, or trying to bring a blanket. By 23–25 months, almost all children expressed concern and helped others, even without encouragement.

A Word of Caution

Even though children as young as 12 months can show empathy, it doesn't mean they will show it all the time. Sometimes young children might not show any empathy at all. Remember — they are still learning how emotions work, and they will get better as they grow.

Baby See, Baby Do!

Babies and toddlers learn by watching and copying you

🎯 Ages: Birth–2 Years
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ Show babies positive examples to imitate — giving hugs, treating others kindly, playing in fun new ways.
  • ✅ Remember that children don't know the difference between what is safe and dangerous. They are watching everything you do.
  • ✅ Celebrate your child's growing memory when they remember how to do new things.
  • ✅ Enjoy the wonderful learning abilities of babies — they might copy any model they see.

Babies Can Remember a Lot

For decades, scientists didn't think babies could remember much. They were wrong. Babies and toddlers can remember a lot — and for a surprisingly long time. After four weeks, 12-month-old babies could still remember and copy about half of the things they were shown — having seen each unique action for only 20 seconds!

Short Fingers and Long Memories

Researchers discovered that babies learn through deferred imitation — they watch what people do, then reproduce it later. Babies can remember what they've seen for much longer than we ever imagined.

Children Learn from Everyone

Every person your baby sees might be teaching them something. Babies copy their parents and caregivers the most, but they can copy anyone — family members, babysitters, neighbours, and even strangers. Everyone can help a child learn by setting good examples.

Social & Emotional

Born to Feel

Before they can even speak, babies have lots of emotions

🎯 Ages: Birth–6 Months
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ Smile, laugh, talk, sing and read together every day.
  • ✅ Play face-to-face games like peek-a-boo. Keep your face about 8–18 inches from your baby's face.
  • ✅ Your baby is reading your face — your angry or sad face will affect how your baby reacts.
  • ✅ Pay attention to your baby's emotional expressions and respond to them.
  • ✅ When you respond to your baby's emotional needs, they learn they can count on you.

Emotions Start Early

Infants are emotional beings right from birth. Emotions are biologically based and built into children's brains, helping them connect to caregivers. Human beings are truly born to feel! Right from birth, infants experience basic emotions — contentment, joy, interest, distress, and disgust. During the first six months, as the infant makes cognitive gains, these basic emotions develop into more sophisticated ones.

The First Six Months of Emotions

By around 3–4 months, sadness emerges. By 6–8 months, fear develops as babies begin recognising familiar and unfamiliar faces. With each passing year their emotional repertoire continues to grow — by ages 2–3, children begin to experience embarrassment, pride, shame, and guilt.

Little Expressions Show Big Feelings

Humans are born to feel, and infant expressions reflect inner emotions. Infants also watch and respond to our emotions. Spend lots of time interacting with your baby, learning what they are trying to tell you about how they feel. You and your baby are discovering more about each other every day — have fun, it will show on your face!

Communication

What a Chatterbox!

Learning new words can happen incredibly quickly

🎯 Ages: 1–2 Years
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ Young children recognise and understand many more words than they can say.
  • ✅ Talk to your child often. Point out objects and describe what you're doing throughout the day.
  • ✅ As children learn new words, they may not say them quite right at first — help by repeating the word so they can hear it again.
  • ✅ Help build vocabulary by adding details — if she says 'ball', add 'Yes, it's a red ball that bounces!'
  • ✅ Tired of talking? Try reading — it's a fun way to stay close to your child.

A Sudden Rush of Words!

Sometime around 18 months, many children experience what researchers call a 'word spurt' — a sudden increase in the rate at which children learn to say new words. Around this age, when many children can say about 50 words, most also begin using new words after hearing them only once. By age two, children typically use around 250–350 words. Just six months later, the word total nearly doubles to about 600 words!

Gaining Insight

Some researchers think that children gain new insights into words and language when they are around 18 months old — coming to understand that words are names for objects, and that every object has a name. Scientists call this the 'naming insight'. When children realise that all objects have names, they also begin sorting similar objects into categories.

How You Can Help

Parents and caregivers play an important role in a child's word learning. Research shows that parents who talk often to their children and use a rich vocabulary to name and describe things help their children develop a larger vocabulary. Learning language is an amazing achievement — celebrate the new words your child learns!

Communication

Keep Talking!

Kids learn language by listening to you every single day

🎯 Ages: All Ages
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ Starting at birth, talk to your child every chance you get.
  • ✅ Describe what you are doing and ask questions. Use short sentences and lots of different words.
  • ✅ Don't expect perfect speech from your toddler or even your 7-year-old. Learning how to make sounds correctly takes time.
  • ✅ Be a good example — if your child mispronounces a word, don't criticise. Instead say it correctly: 'Yes, that's a rabbit!'
  • ✅ Sing, tell stories, and read to your child.
  • ✅ If you're concerned your child has difficulty with speech, talk to your paediatrician.

Building Language Every Day

Learning to talk is harder than it seems. Remember this simple rule: the more you talk, the more they learn. Babies and young children learn to speak by listening to you, so start talking to them from the day they are born!

Make the Most of Everyday Moments

Use everyday events like bathing, getting dressed, going for a walk, grocery shopping, or making dinner as a chance to talk with your child. Playfully tell them what you are doing. Ask questions. Point out interesting things. What you talk about isn't as important as how much you talk.

More Words Each Day

When kids start to speak they often blend words into one big sound. By four, they are saying more and more words correctly and learning to play with language through rhymes, songs, and familiar sayings. By seven, children can say most words as well as adults — but they still have a lot to learn!

Tell Me a Story!

Stories connect you to your child and open up their world

🎯 Ages: 2–5 Years
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ Have fun with storytelling — be creative!
  • ✅ Your home is full of stories about things they can see, hear, and touch.
  • ✅ Pay close attention to what your child says and follow their lead.
  • ✅ Children love hearing stories about when they were babies.
  • ✅ Be sure to tell favourite stories again and again.
  • ✅ Show them you know how to listen too!

Stories Are a Great Way to Connect

Stories connect you to your children and them to the world around them. Everyday conversations are a type of story too. When you talk about your child's world, it sparks their interest. They learn how to have a conversation and build vocabulary. Storytelling teaches them new words, and how to listen and pay attention.

Make Your Child the Star of the Story

Children love being part of the story. Try making up a story describing their day as if they were a make-believe character. As your child grows, share all kinds of stories — about you, and make-believe ones too. Let your imagination go and encourage your child to add to the story or create their own.

Use Stories to Help Deal with Feelings

Stories can help a child understand situations or deal with feelings. If a child is upset, you can share a time when you were upset, or use pretend characters in a similar situation and talk about how they dealt with their feelings. Taking the time to share personal stories and listen to your child's stories tells them you care and value their feelings.

Copycats!

Young children learn by watching everything you do

🎯 Ages: Birth–2 Years
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ Treat your home as your child's first classroom, and everybody in your home as your child's first teachers.
  • ✅ Remember that babies watch and might copy the things we do right from birth. Provide positive examples early on.
  • ✅ Be careful about what your child sees on television. Babies don't know what's safe to do and what's dangerous.
  • ✅ Include your child in your everyday activities whenever you can.
  • ✅ Be careful what you say and how you say it — little kids will repeat everything!

The World's Best Copycats

Babies are born learning from you. Even at birth, infants can watch what you do and copy it. When you smile, babies try to smile too. If you stick your tongue out at a newborn, the baby will stick their tongue out too. Researchers have found that babies as young as 42 minutes old will copy an adult who sticks out their tongue!

Children Learn from Everyone

Every person your baby sees might be teaching them something. Young children learn by watching and copying family members, babysitters, neighbours, and even strangers. Everyone can help a child learn by setting good examples.

What About Television?

Children also learn what people do by watching television. Research shows that even 14-month-old children will copy some of the things they see on television. Young children are quick learners — and that makes it important to be careful about what children are watching.

Social & Emotional

Getting in Tune With Baby

Learning to read your baby's signals builds a lifetime of communication

🎯 Ages: Birth–1 Year
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ Play lots of face-to-face games with your baby. Babies love to see your facial expressions.
  • ✅ Try to follow your baby's lead. If they're interested in you, keep playing!
  • ✅ Try to be sensitive to cues that your baby needs a break. If they turn away, be patient and wait.
  • ✅ Take the daily schedule into account — how long since they were fed? Do they need a nap?
  • ✅ Don't expect to 'read' every sign or cue. If you're trying, you're probably doing a great job!

How Do I Know What My Baby Wants?

Everyone knows that babies cry when they are upset. They smile when they're happy. But what else can babies tell us, even though they can't talk yet? When we pay close attention, babies can tell us a lot. Your baby has their own special signals and cues they use to communicate their wants and needs. When you respond to these signals and follow your baby's lead, you help them develop in healthy ways.

Responding is Important

Infants mostly use facial expressions to interact with you — from big smiles and wide eyes to frowns and angry faces. They also use body language, like turning away when they need a break or reaching out when they want something. When parents read their babies' signals and respond to them, babies become more secure and learn to express their needs more clearly.

Signals Your Baby Might Use

'I'm interested' looks like wide bright eyes, smiling, and reaching. 'I'm hungry' looks like rooting, sucking on their shirt, or putting things in their mouth. 'I'm sleepy' looks like rubbing their eyes, pulling an ear, or getting fussy. 'I need a break' looks like turning away, arching their back, or closing their eyes.

Social & Emotional

Give Your Child Wings!

Build a secure connection your child can always count on

🎯 Ages: 1–3 Years
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ When a child knows you'll be there, they feel secure.
  • ✅ When a child feels secure, they'll be ready to explore.
  • ✅ Let your child be independent and try things on their own.
  • ✅ Encourage them with positive words like 'good job!'
  • ✅ Stay close and respond quickly to your child's needs.
  • ✅ Honour their feelings and listen carefully to their words.

Why is Connection So Important?

The world can be a very scary place for a toddler, unless they know you'll be there when they need you. Knowing they can count on you makes them feel safe. The more connected toddlers are with their parents, the more secure they feel. This is called 'secure attachment' — and it builds from trust, not just physical closeness.

How Does Secure Attachment Happen?

It's all up to you as a parent. Being there when they need you is the first step. It begins when you respond quickly and with kindness to your baby. This builds a trust and a bond. Each time you respond in a caring way, your child feels more connected — whether you are soothing them for a bump on the knee or helping with frustration over a toy.

How Do You Know When to Help?

Every child is different. Some want help at first and others don't. Your job is to stay in tune with your child's feelings. If something scary happens, they may run back to you for reassurance. The best thing you can do is comfort them, listen carefully, and give them time to recover. Before you know it they will be off playing again!

Here Comes a Tantrum!

Tantrums are a normal part of child development — stay close and stay calm

🎯 Ages: 1–4 Years
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ Take a deep breath and try to stay calm. Getting upset doesn't help and likely makes things worse.
  • ✅ Stay close. Bend down or sit on the floor. It may take a few minutes for them to settle down.
  • ✅ If your child has hit someone or damaged something, let them know that behaviour is not okay.
  • ✅ Once your child has settled down, talk about what happened.
  • ✅ Tantrums are a normal part of child development and a signal of your child's growing independence. They usually disappear by age 4 or 5.

When You Least Expect It…

Tantrums happen! No matter how hard you try to understand your child's needs, sometimes you just can't prevent a kid from kicking, screaming, and crying. It's not fun for you or your child — but take heart, tantrums are a normal part of typical child development, and most parents have been in your shoes!

What Causes a Meltdown?

It's often hard to pinpoint the cause of a meltdown. Your toddler truly wants to be independent, but their skills don't yet match their desires. They are learning to handle a lot of different emotions, and also being hungry, tired, bored, or overwhelmed. The good news is tantrums do not last forever — usually by age 4 or 5, they seldom occur.

Stay Close and Stay Calm

How Young Children Learn

From birth to age five, children learn an astonishing amount about how the world works. Young children learn through a process called hypothesis testing — the same method the world's leading scientists use.

What's the Science of Peek-a-boo?

Peek-a-boo seems obvious to adults — nobody's afraid the baby might disappear forever just because they're out of view. But from a young child's point of view, when an adult hides their face, the baby thinks about the situation very differently — the adult might really be gone! Peek-a-boo helps children learn that something can be hidden from view but still exist in the world. Each game builds the concept of object permanence.

Babies Learn by Testing

Babies love to make things happen. In research studies, when babies discovered that kicking their foot made a mobile move, they kept kicking! They learned they could make the mobile move by kicking — and one discovery leads to a handful of new ideas to test. Young children are active learners who observe, ask questions, make predictions, and test their ideas.

Parentese is used around the world, regardless of culture or native tongue. It has been documented in speakers of English, German, Russian, Swedish, and even Mandarin Chinese. Does Parentese Help Infants?

Researchers have found that infants prefer to hear parentese over adult conversation. When babies were allowed to choose between tapes of adult-to-adult speech and adult-to-infant speech, babies consistently chose to hear the speech directed toward infants — and this was true even when the speaker spoke in a foreign language! Babies simply enjoy the higher-pitched sounds and exaggerated speech patterns of parentese.

Tell Me a Story! (Research)

Sharing stories helps young children and parents connect deeply

🎯 Ages: 1–5 Years
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ Keep stories and conversations fun and interesting.
  • ✅ Tune into your child's attention and interest. If they become bored or distracted, move on to a different subject.
  • ✅ Curiosity will lead children in many directions — let them choose the subjects you discuss as much as possible.
  • ✅ Children love make-believe stories, especially when they get to be the star!
  • ✅ Encourage imagination with open-ended starters like 'I wonder what it would feel like to be a frog?'
  • ✅ Be creative — storytelling can take many forms, including retelling a favourite story, singing together, or telling knock-knock jokes.

Stories Are Filled with Lots of New Words

Sharing stories is a way to build vocabulary, improve language skills, and develop the love of learning. These are the foundations of reading and writing well. Your home is a great place to share stories and conversations. Infants and toddlers are curious about everything that goes on around them. Talking about a dream, a play date, or what happened today builds language skills and helps them understand how things work.

From a Picture Comes a Story

'Honey, do you know who that is? That's Grammy when she was a little girl.' Your daughter looks surprised. 'Grammy's horse?' 'Yes, that's Grammy's horse Dakota. She rode her every day. What do you think it would feel like to ride a horse?' Sharing stories is as easy as that — the mother noticed her child's fascination and took the opportunity to tell a story, using language to add to her daughter's understanding.

Build on Your Child's Questions

A curious child is interested in learning. So try to notice what they notice. If they are interested in something, talk about it, take them to the library for books on their favourite topic. When you encourage their interest, you build a love of learning. Sharing stories is a wonderful way to make connections with your child.

Testing 1, 2, 3…

Is your toddler getting into everything? They're a curious scientist!

🎯 Ages: 1–3 Years
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ Expect your toddler to test you often. This is normal — they are curious and learning how to react when they try something you don't want them to do.
  • ✅ Every time your child tests you, try to respond in a consistent and loving way. Clear and firm boundaries for behaviour are best.
  • ✅ Provide safe ways for your toddler to explore and test.
  • ✅ When you are tired of them dumping cereal on the floor, redirect the exploration — fill a low drawer in the kitchen with plastic containers, spoons, and measuring cups.
  • ✅ Remember that your curious child is testing everything — including you. Hang in there! The 'terrible twos' don't last forever.

Is Your Toddler Getting Into Everything?

Has your 'little angel' started colouring on your walls? Does they throw cereal off the high chair even though you tell them not to? Chances are, you're the proud parent of a 2-year-old! People call this time the 'terrible twos' — because 2-year-olds do lots of things adults don't want them to do. Sometimes they do things just because their parents tell them not to.

Testing Time

Your child is growing up. Their brain is developing. At about 18 months, toddlers begin to understand that people think differently and like different things. They start learning what happens when people want different things — and that's when the testing begins. Why do children do these things? Are they trying to make you mad?

Your Child is Not a Monster!

In fact, this behaviour is normal for toddlers. They don't know if you will get mad or not. That's the point. They are testing you so they can learn. What can you do? Be consistent. Set clear boundaries for behaviour and stick to them, every time. By holding your ground, you teach your child that there are firm limits — and that you can be trusted to stay calm and take action.

Testing 1, 2, 3 (Research)

The age of two is an essential time of exploration for young minds

🎯 Ages: 1–3 Years
💡 Helpful Tips for Parents
  • ✅ Understand that young children are constantly testing their environment — these children aren't bad, they're curious.
  • ✅ Provide consistent responses. When children test their hypotheses, they are gathering evidence about how people deal with conflict.
  • ✅ Don't let hypothesis testing escalate your emotions. Keep emotions in check.
  • ✅ Focus on the positive aspects of this age — toddlers are learning how conflict gets resolved.
  • ✅ If surrounded by healthy, consistent role models, children can learn how to handle future disagreements in constructive ways.
  • ✅ Hang in there! The age of two doesn't last forever.

They Want to Learn How Conflict Works

By the age of two, children are trying to understand conflict. At younger ages, children believe that their desires are the same as everyone else's. But at around 18 months, children begin to understand an important new concept — people have different desires. In one creative experiment using crackers and broccoli, 14-month-olds gave researchers crackers (what they themselves preferred), while 18-month-olds gave researchers broccoli (what the researcher had shown she preferred) — they understood that the adult wanted something different!

How Children Learn Through Hypothesis Testing

To learn about conflict, two-year-old toddlers launch a series of experiments. This process is called hypothesis testing, and it involves: making observations, asking questions, creating hypotheses (guessing what will happen), testing hypotheses, and evaluating the evidence. Your child is not trying to drive you crazy — they are genuinely trying to understand how the world and the people in it work.

Their Testing Includes You!

Primary caregivers are the main test subjects! While two-year-olds' behaviour is difficult to deal with at times, these children actually have good reasons for their actions. They are learning what happens when people want different things. Your job is to keep them safe, be consistent in your response, and help them learn what's right and what's wrong. They will learn as much from your response as they do from their little experiment.
